\section{Introduction}

Symmetries are central to modern physics. Lorentz invariance underpins
special relativity, while local gauge symmetries such as $\mathrm{SU}(2)$
govern the structure of the electroweak interaction. When neural networks 
are used to represent physical fields, a generic architecture does not 
automatically respect these symmetries and can only learn them approximately 
from data. Following the coursework, this report studies two pre-trained networks,
NN1 and NN2, that approximate an $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ scalar doublet and a
gauge--boson field respectively. We first verify their transformation
properties analytically and numerically (Sections~1--2), then construct
alternative architectures in which equivariance is enforced exactly by
restricting the network input to Lorentz invariants (Section~3). Finally,
we build a classical Lagrangian from these equivariant fields and confirm
its invariance under both Lorentz and $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ transformations
(Section~4).

\section{Q1: Equivariance of NN1: SU(2) Fundamental and Lorentz Scalar}

NN1 maps spacetime coordinates $x = (t,x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^4$ to four
real outputs which we package as a complex SU(2) doublet
$\Phi = (\phi_1 + i\phi_2,\; \phi_3 + i\phi_4)^T \in \mathbb{C}^2$.

\textbf{Lorentz scalar (analytical).}
A scalar field satisfies $\Phi(\Lambda x) = \Phi(x)$ for all Lorentz
transformations $\Lambda$. Unlike the SU(2) case this is a non-trivial
constraint: it requires the network to depend only on Lorentz-invariant
input combinations such as $x_\mu x^\mu = t^2 - x^2 - y^2 - z^2$.

\textbf{Lorentz scalar (numerical).}
We test 500 random points under 50 random SO(3) rotations and Lorentz
boosts, filtering for $|\Phi| > 0.1$ to avoid inflated relative errors.
The mean relative errors are approximately 5.6\% for rotations and 4.2\%
for boosts, and the max relative errors are both approximately 6.6\%, confirming
that NN1 is an approximate Lorentz scalar.

\textbf{SU(2) fundamental (analytical).}
Under a global transformation $U \in \mathrm{SU}(2)$ the doublet
transforms as $\Phi \to U\Phi$. Because SU(2) is an internal symmetry it
acts only on the field components and not on the coordinates, so the doublet
structure follows from how we package the outputs and places no constraint
on the network weights. Unitarity ($U^\dagger U = I$) guarantees that
$\Phi^\dagger\Phi$ is preserved.

\textbf{SU(2) fundamental (numerical).}
We evaluate NN1 at sample points, apply random $U \in \mathrm{SU}(2)$ to
the output doublet, and compare $\Phi^\dagger\Phi$ before and after.
Differences are of order $10^{-9}$ (Table~\ref{tab:q1_su2}), consistent
with float32 precision.

\section{Q2: Equivariance of NN2: SU(2) Adjoint and Spin-1}

NN2 maps spacetime coordinates $x \in \mathbb{R}^4$ to 12 real outputs,
which we reshape into a $4 \times 3$ matrix $W_\mu^{\,a}(x)$ where
$\mu = 0,1,2,3$ is the Lorentz index and $a = 1,2,3$ is the SU(2)
adjoint index.

\textbf{Lorentz spin-1 (analytical).}
A Lorentz vector field transforms as
$W_\mu^{\,a}(\Lambda x) = \Lambda_\mu^{\ \nu}\,W_\nu^{\,a}(x)$, i.e.\
the Lorentz transformation acts on the $\mu$ index while the SU(2) index
$a$ is left untouched. This is the defining property of a spin-1 field.

\textbf{Lorentz spin-1 (numerical).}
We test 500 random points under 50 random rotations and boosts, filtering
for $|W| > 0.1$. The mean relative errors are approximately 23\% for
rotations and 14\% for boosts, and the max relative errors are approximately 
28\% and 27\%, respectively. The error grows monotonically with 
the transformation angle (Figure~\ref{fig:q2_angle}), confirming that the 
network obeys the symmetry more accurately for small transformations.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.47\textwidth]{../figures/q2_angle.png}
    \caption{Relative error in $W_\mu^{\,a}(\Lambda x)$ vs transformation angle.}
    \label{fig:q2_angle}
\end{figure}

\textbf{SU(2) adjoint (analytical).}
Under a global $U \in \mathrm{SU}(2)$ the adjoint representation acts via
a real $3 \times 3$ rotation
$R^{ab} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{Tr}(\sigma^a\, U\, \sigma^b\, U^\dagger)$,
where $\sigma^a$ are the Pauli matrices. The field transforms as
$W_\mu^{\,a} \to R^{ab}\,W_\mu^{\,b}$. As with Q1, SU(2) is internal and
does not act on the coordinates, so this transformation law follows from
how we identify the second index with the Lie algebra basis. The matrix $R$
is automatically a proper rotation ($\det R = 1$, $RR^T = I$), so the
adjoint invariant $W_\mu^{\,a}\,W^{\mu\,a}$ is preserved.

\textbf{SU(2) adjoint (numerical).}
We verify that $R \in \mathrm{SO}(3)$ and that
$W_\mu^{\,a}\,W^{\mu\,a}$ is unchanged under random adjoint rotations.
Differences are of order $10^{-8}$ (Table~\ref{tab:q2_su2}), confirming
the invariant is preserved to machine precision.

\section{Q3: Construction and Training of Equivariant Networks}

The key idea is to build the symmetry into the network architecture so
that it holds exactly, rather than relying on weight tuning to learn it
approximately from data.

\subsection*{Analytical form}
\mbox{}\\
We construct equivariant models of NN1 and NN2, denoted
EquivariantPhi and EquivariantW, respectively.

\textbf{EquivariantPhi.}
The only Lorentz-invariant scalar that can be formed from a single
spacetime point is $s = x_\mu x^\mu = t^2 - x^2 - y^2 - z^2$. We
therefore define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eq_phi}
\Phi(x) = \mathrm{MLP}(s) \in \mathbb{R}^4.
\end{equation}
Since $s$ is invariant under Lorentz transformations,
$\Phi(\Lambda x) = \mathrm{MLP}(s(\Lambda x)) = \mathrm{MLP}(s(x))
= \Phi(x)$ by construction. The four real outputs are packaged as an
SU(2) doublet exactly as in Section~1.

\textbf{EquivariantW.}
The only Lorentz 4-vector available from a single point is $x_\mu$ itself.
The most general equivariant ansatz is therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eq_W}
W_\mu^{\,a}(x) = f^a(s)\,x_\mu,
\end{equation}
where $f^a(s)$ is an MLP mapping $s \to \mathbb{R}^3$ (three adjoint
components). Under a Lorentz transformation,
$W_\mu^{\,a}(\Lambda x) = f^a(s)\,(\Lambda x)_\mu
= \Lambda_\mu^{\ \nu}\,f^a(s)\,x_\nu
= \Lambda_\mu^{\ \nu}\,W_\nu^{\,a}(x)$,
which is the correct spin-1 transformation law, holding exactly by
construction. The SU(2) adjoint structure is inherited from the index $a$
as in Section~2.

\subsection*{Implementation and results}
\mbox{}\\
Both MLPs have three hidden layers of 128 units with ReLU activations
($\sim$34k parameters each, compared to $\sim$50k for the originals).
Training data is generated by evaluating NN1 and NN2 on 30{,}000 random
points drawn uniformly from $[-5, 5]^4$, with 5{,}000 held out for
validation. We train with Adam (initial learning rate $10^{-3}$) and
ReduceLROnPlateau scheduling for 150 epochs, selecting the checkpoint with
the lowest validation MSE loss (Figure~\ref{fig:q3_training}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../figures/q3_training.png}
    \caption{Training curves for EquivariantPhi and EquivariantW.}
    \label{fig:q3_training}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Verification of exact equivariance.}
We repeat the numerical tests from Sections~1--2 on the new architectures.
Lorentz equivariance errors are now of order $10^{-7}$
(Table~\ref{tab:q3_equivariance}), consistent with float32 precision and
confirming that the symmetry is exact by construction. This represents an
improvement of five to six orders of magnitude over the original networks.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Lorentz equivariance errors: original vs equivariant architectures.}
    \label{tab:q3_equivariance}
    \begin{tabular}{cccc}
    Model & Transformation & Original & Equivariant \\
    \hline
    $\Phi$ & SO(3) rotation & 0.0562 & 1.99e-07 \\
    $\Phi$ & Lorentz boost & 0.0416 & 2.60e-07 \\
    $W$ & SO(3) rotation & 0.2302 & 3.45e-07 \\
    $W$ & Lorentz boost & 0.1415 & 4.80e-07
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Emulation quality.}
On the validation set, EquivariantPhi achieves a mean relative error of
5.3\% (median 3.5\%), while EquivariantW gives 30.9\% (median 15.7\%).
The higher error for $W$ is expected: the ansatz in
Equation~\eqref{eq:eq_W} is the most general exactly equivariant form,
but the original NN2 is only approximately equivariant. The
non-equivariant components of NN2 cannot be captured by an exactly
equivariant network, so a residual emulation error is unavoidable.

\section{Q4: Invariant Lagrangian Construction}

\subsection*{Analytic form}
\mbox{}\\
We construct a classical Lagrangian with kinetic and mass terms for both
fields:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lagrangian}
\mathcal{L} = \partial_\mu \Phi^\dagger \partial^\mu \Phi
            - m_\Phi^2\,\Phi^\dagger \Phi
            - \tfrac{1}{4}\,G_{\mu\nu}^a\,G^{\mu\nu\,a}
            + \tfrac{1}{2}\,m_W^2\,W_\mu^a\,W^{\mu\,a},
\end{equation}
%
where $G_{\mu\nu}^a = \partial_\mu W_\nu^a - \partial_\nu W_\mu^a$ is the
Abelian field-strength tensor (we omit the non-Abelian
$\epsilon^{abc}W_\mu^b W_\nu^c$ term for simplicity).

\textbf{Lorentz invariance.}
Every Lorentz index is contracted with the Minkowski metric
$\eta^{\mu\nu}$. Since $\Phi$ is a scalar and $W_\mu^a$ is a vector, each
term is manifestly a Lorentz scalar.

\textbf{SU(2) invariance.}
$\Phi^\dagger\Phi$ is invariant under $\Phi \to U\Phi$ by unitarity. The
adjoint index $a$ is contracted with $\delta^{ab}$, which is invariant
under $W^a \to R^{ab}W^b$ by orthogonality of the adjoint rotation $R$.

\subsection*{Implementation and results}
\mbox{}\\
The equivariant networks from Section~3 output $W^{\mu}_a$ with an upper
Lorentz index. We lower with the metric,
$W_\nu^a = \eta_{\nu\mu}\,W^{\mu}_a$, before forming $G_{\mu\nu}^a$.
All spacetime derivatives are computed exactly via PyTorch autograd. To
ensure well-defined higher-order derivatives we retrain the equivariant
networks from Section~3 with SiLU activations in place of ReLU,
achieving comparable emulation quality
(Figure~\ref{fig:q4_training}). We set $m_\Phi = m_W = 1$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../figures/q4_training.png}
    \caption{Training curves for SiLU equivariant networks.}
    \label{fig:q4_training}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Lorentz invariance (numerical).}
We evaluate $\mathcal{L}(x)$ and $\mathcal{L}(\Lambda x)$ at 15 random
points under random SO(3) rotations and Lorentz boosts. The mean relative
errors are $1.7 \times 10^{-7}$ and $2.7 \times 10^{-7}$ respectively, 
confirming that the Lagrangian is a Lorentz scalar to machine precision.

\textbf{SU(2) invariance (numerical).}
Since SU(2) is a global internal symmetry, we verify that the individual
invariant combinations entering $\mathcal{L}$ are preserved under
transformation. Under random $U \in \mathrm{SU}(2)$, the maximum
deviations in $\Phi^\dagger\Phi$ and $W_\mu^a\,W^{\mu\,a}$ are
$4.1 \times 10^{-8}$ and $2.5 \times 10^{-7}$ respectively, consistent 
with float32 precision. This is expected since the Lagrangian depends on 
the fields only through $\Phi^\dagger\Phi$ (unitary invariant) and 
$\delta_{ab}$ contractions (adjoint invariant), so SU(2) invariance holds 
as a mathematical identity.
